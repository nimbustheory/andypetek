Whether you're a drinker of beer or not, you've likely seen in the news or in your local store a change in the landscape of craft brews. As we roll into 2026, new numbers have published looking at the overall trends in the beverage industry and which categories are hurting the worst. Beer is especially near and dear to me because it's the one I work in most often and the one I have personally seen the most change in.

Positive words are hard to craft when the numbers tell a sobering story. According to the Brewers Association's 2025 year-end report, craft beer volume dropped an estimated 5% compared to 2024, with overall beer volumes falling 4.2% in the first half of the year. For the second consecutive year, brewery closures outpaced openings—434 breweries shuttered their doors in 2025 while only 268 opened. The number of operating craft breweries in the U.S. fell to 9,778, down from 9,352 just a year prior. After decades of seemingly unstoppable growth, the craft beer revolution has hit a wall.

But these aren't just statistics on a page. They represent taprooms going dark, brewing equipment being auctioned off, and communities losing gathering places that had become part of their identity. For those of us who've spent our careers in the beverage industry, watching this transformation unfold has been both professionally challenging and personally bittersweet.

## Notes From Close to Home

For the last few years, I've been based in Seattle and Portland. Both Washington and Oregon are hotbeds for the craft beer community and tend to have strong public support for the products and the sense of tribe around the businesses that make them. Many saw the overnight fall of Rogue Brewing in Oregon as a big surprise. On a Friday morning in mid-November, without warning, staff received a message through the company's scheduling app that the business was closed for good. Five facilities, 37 years in business, all shuttered—with over $545,000 owed in rent to the Port of Newport alone. The Chapter 7 bankruptcy filing days later revealed the full picture: more than $16.7 million in total debt.

A company I was consulting for closed 15 tasting room locations and fired nearly 150 employees over an 18 month period. Same story. Secret debts, over-stacked liabilities, good people left in the cold. After a decade in business, it will end in dust. Having fired more than 50 of those employees myself in the early days of the consolidation and several more in month of turbulent turnover leading up to it, the feeling was brutal. Frankly, it messed with my own mental health firing 40 people two weeks before Christmas 2024. My contract was terminated by April 2025 and the rest of the company fell by the holidays of '25.

In Seattle alone, several of the region's biggest and most important breweries have changed hands—and many more have seen consolidation. New management and ownership isn't necessarily a sign of poor performance or impending doom, but the concentration of transactions is notable. Seattle Hospitality Group, a local hospitality company, acquired Pike Brewing in 2021 and followed up by purchasing Fremont Brewing, Washington's third-largest brewery, in 2024. In November 2025, they announced the closure of Pike's storied downtown location near Pike Place Market after 35 years. Meanwhile, Ackley Brands—a local family-owned beverage company—has been on an acquisition spree, picking up Mac and Jack's in 2023, Columbia Winery and Charles Smith Wines in 2024, and Silver City in 2025. Their portfolio now includes Washington's oldest winery (Columbia, founded 1962), the Oregon pinot noir producer Montinore (their founding acquisition in 2016), and legacy brands that have bounced between Constellation, Gallo, and The Wine Group before landing with a local operator. Again, not a negative change, just a notable one. Having brands owned by local companies and beverage veterans with proven history can be a great thing.

Also notable in late 2025 was the sale of Ste. Michelle Wine Estates, Washington's largest wine producer. The company had been owned by private equity firm Sycamore Partners since 2021, when they purchased it from Altria for $1.2 billion. In December 2025, Sycamore sold the struggling wine giant to the Wyckoff family, multi-generational grape growers and winemakers based in Grandview, Washington. The sale—reportedly for a fraction of what Sycamore paid, if any cash changed hands at all—marks the first time in over 50 years that Ste. Michelle has been locally owned.

The wine industry is a story for another day. These changes are only a few and only examples from Washington. You can imagine what is happening around the United States and beyond. For some breweries tariffs have impacted the supply chain, for others rapid changes in the economy have contributed, and for others the change in our workforce due to AI and other shift can be noted. Back to Seattle for another quick example. When you see on national news that Amazon is laying off 12,000 employees in the area, those aren't warehouse or delivery employees (those are needed more than ever). Those consolidated were (largely) corporate staff. With other major layoffs from Microsoft, Starbucks, Boeing and others in the market, the job ecosystem is intense. You have business leaders with 15 years of experience and $150,000+ in salary expectation seeking blue collar jobs laboring for half that. That's only an example to show the scale of change. People at all levels of the professional workforce and socio-economic spectrum are being impacted. Seattle also has the highest minimum wage in the country, nearing $21. More popular restaurants are closing than every before at the same time.

## My Own Business

I've been running a beverage marketing agency and management consultancy for about 12 years now. I've worked inside of dozens of companies in times of growth, consolidation, or transition. From overhauling systems and workforces to everyday branding and package design, these shift have definitely changed my business as well. Businesses aren't spending on as many new products and releases. Focus has to be on the taproom and physical storefront over retail launches. The expectation of having live music, trivia, food trucks, and entertainment every night to keep customers coming, while doing all of that with minimal cost, is a constant.

I did fewer design projects in 2025 than the 10 years prior. Budgets are smaller and many want to turn to AI or overseas contractors that can turn out something passable for a fraction of the cost. Thankfully for us, we work in several areas and have been able to work in areas like events and distribution when design and development fall behind.

## But the Big Question Is: Why Are We Here?

There are three main reasons:

### 1. The Sober Curious Surge

The culprits are multiple, but one factor stands above the rest: Americans are simply drinking less. Nearly half of Americans—49%—reported they're trying to drink less alcohol in 2025, a stunning 44% increase from 2023, according to research from NCSolutions. Dry January participation jumped 36% year-over-year, and this isn't just a January phenomenon anymore. The "sober curious" movement, a term coined by writer Ruby Warrington in her 2018 book, has evolved from a wellness trend into a permanent cultural shift.

Generation Z is leading this charge with remarkable enthusiasm. Roughly four in ten Gen Zers declared their intention to go fully sober in 2025—up dramatically from the 19% who said they abstained in 2024. Only 39% of Gen Z consumers say alcohol is their go-to social drink, compared to 57% of Gen X. This generational gap represents nothing less than a fundamental redefinition of what it means to socialize in America.

The reasons are varied: 53% of young adults cite personal choice, while others point to health consciousness, financial considerations, and a cultural environment where being sober can actually be seen as aspirational rather than unusual. Social media and health influencers have normalized mindful drinking, with 52% of Gen Z and millennials saying they're likely to participate in the sober curious movement in 2025 after learning about it online.

### 2. The Non-Alcoholic Boom

What's remarkable isn't just that people are drinking less—it's what they're drinking instead. The non-alcoholic beer market has exploded, with the Beer Institute reporting NA beer sales surging 22.2% year-to-date in 2025. On-premise NA beer sales climbed 26.4%. The global non-alcoholic beer market hit $24 billion in 2025, with the U.S. accounting for $6.4 billion of that total.

This isn't your father's O'Doul's. Brands like Athletic Brewing Company have revolutionized the category, earning shelf space in mainstream retailers and proving that non-alcoholic craft beer can deliver genuine flavor experiences. Practically every major beer brand—from Guinness to Heineken to Budweiser—has rolled out zero-proof versions, lending credibility and acceptability to a category that once occupied a lonely corner of the cooler.

According to one industry analysis, non-alcoholic beer is on pace to surpass traditional ale in sales volume. Over half of Gen Z drinkers report "often or sometimes" choosing non-alcoholic beers, mocktails, or low-ABV cocktails when socializing, with another 22% saying they're curious to try these alternatives. The message is clear: the next generation doesn't view alcohol as essential to the drinking occasion.

### 3. Competition from All Sides

Beer isn't just losing drinkers to sobriety—it's losing them to other categories entirely. The ready-to-drink cocktail market is projected to grow from $860 million in 2024 to $2.66 billion by 2033, a compound annual growth rate of 13.44%. RTD cocktails and spirit-based seltzers are expected to overtake hard seltzers in volume by the end of 2025, with consumers increasingly trading up to premium, spirit-based options that deliver bar-quality experiences in convenient packaging.

Hard seltzers, despite showing signs of maturation after their explosive growth phase, remain a fixture at $4 billion in 2025 market value. The canned alcoholic beverages market overall is projected to reach $32.62 billion in 2025. All of this represents occasions that once belonged to beer.

The data from Escoffier's consumer research paints a stark picture: beer consumption now sits at an all-time low, with only 34% of consumers favoring beer compared to the historical 41%. Ready-to-drink beverages have captured consumer imagination—89% of consumers now prefer RTD beverages when reaching for convenient alcohol options.

## The Path Forward

For brewers navigating these headwinds, adaptation isn't optional. Many craft breweries are diversifying beyond beer production entirely, adding spirits, non-alcoholic options, and ready-to-drink products to their portfolios. The Brewers Association notes that while taprooms and brewpubs represent just 15% of overall industry volume, they account for 73% of craft businesses—suggesting that the hyperlocal, experience-driven model may offer more resilience than distribution-focused approaches.

Breweries are not just the creators of some of America's favorite libations. They are also some of our favorite places to hang out and meet people. They are important members the local community and meaningful contributors to the economy. Trends will continue to change and the world will continue to evolve. There have been so many "bubbles" and shifts to more drinking, less drinking, sweeter products, drier products, and everything in between. By now we know seltzers, but we've forgotten about hard root beer. You probably don't remember when the 90's wine cooler was rebranded as a 2010's "spritzer." Alternative sweeteners and ingredient preferences change with dieting fads. Breweries will stay and will thrive for another day. But they will have to be smart. The days of relying on stellar product to speak for itself are gone. The show has to be a spectacle and everything has to be social media-worthy.

Many next-generation consumers aren't drinking unless they're at a sporting event, a music concert, or a night club. Those venues don't often have craft beer. The stores are consolidating the number of products they carry and leaning toward larger multi-packs over single-serve bottles. The in-person taproom experience and the necessity to be a hospitality business over a manufacturing business is paramount.

What will happen in 2026? We will see. I have some suspicions and predictions I'll share in future writing. Until then, don't forget to raise a glass to your favorite brewery. Show them love for all the joy they've brought you over the year. It makes more of a difference than you know.
