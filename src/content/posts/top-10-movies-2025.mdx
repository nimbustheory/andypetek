Now that we've entered 2026, it's time to finally release my list of top movies from 2025. First let's look at my final viewing tally:

- **234 films watched**
- **219 different films** (some seen twice)
- **104 films released in 2025**
- **112 films seen in the theater** (89 at Regal…shout out to Regal Unlimited)

I will start by saying these are my FAVORITE films of the year. Not what I would list as the best in my opinion or what I predict for awards season. Simply my favorite. There are still several awards contenders I have to see and some critical favorites I need to catch up on. These are the movies that moved and inspired me in a big year of ups and downs for the film industry and the business of cinema.

---

## 1. One Battle After Another

I believe there is no doubt this is the best movie of the year. It will join the pantheon of the best of the century and I believe it's going to win EVERY award. Paul Thomas Anderson returns to form with a sprawling, kinetic epic that critics everywhere adore. Leonardo DiCaprio delivers a carefully modulated performance as a washed-up revolutionary, while Sean Penn reminds everyone why he's one of our greatest screen villains—shot in lustrous VistaVision by cinematographer Darius Khondji with Jonny Greenwood's propulsive score driving every frame forward like a runaway train. Above and below the line technical and creative talent in this film left me speechless. I have so much to say, but this list can't be 50 pages long. See it!

## 2. Marty Supreme

The year ended on a high note with some silly comedies, animation domination, and the indie darling that everyone hoped Marty Supreme would be. It delivered. In a fast-paced and visceral run around New York and the world, Josh Safdie crafts a 1950s ping-pong hustler portrait that's equal parts Uncut Gems chaos and screwball romance, with Timothée Chalamet delivering another knockout performance on his path to greatness after six years of actual table tennis training. Darius Khondji's grainy 35mm cinematography and vintage anamorphic lenses transport you straight to smoky Manhattan basements that pop into vivid reality with 80's-inspired needle drops and a thrill of a score.

## 3. 28 Years Later

A smaller movie with smaller cameras that never stops feeling HUGE in the stakes for the characters and the viewers. Danny Boyle reunites with cinematographer Anthony Dod Mantle to reinvent the zombie genre yet again—this time shooting on iPhone 15s with a custom bullet-time rig that makes the infected feel terrifyingly immediate. Jodie Comer anchors the emotional journey through this coming-of-age fairy tale set against apocalyptic Britain, proving that twenty-eight years later, Boyle still knows how to make the undead feel uncomfortably alive. The franchise lives on and many, myself included, can't wait to see the next installment "Bone Temple" in 2026 followed by the third movie that has already been green lit.

## 4. Splitsville

This film had me laughing the entire way. It didn't get nearly enough love for how absurd, but real it is. Michael Angelo Covino and Kyle Marvin deliver the funniest, sharpest romantic comedy in years. Dakota Johnson and Adria Arjona both crushed and could've done even more. A silly buddy turned frenemy turned buddy again movie, this film somehow has elaborate fight choreography and stunts that are as artful as they are hilarious. Adam Newport-Berra's visually inventive cinematography matches the film's gleefully chaotic energy—proof that modern screwball comedy might not be dead. I will always ride for them!

## 5. F1

The spectacle of the season. High speed, high stakes, high adrenaline. Joseph Kosinski does for Formula 1 what he did for fighter jets—creating what critics are calling the best racing footage ever committed to film, shot by Claudio Miranda during actual Grand Prix weekends on the F1 paddock. I love Formula 1 as much as I love a Hans Zimmer score. He's sure been busy the last few years and this one will be no exception for work and for awards. The engine-roar score rattles your chest. Brad Pitt brings laidback magnetism to his aging driver role, reminding us that sometimes the best blockbusters don't reinvent the wheel, they just spin it better than anyone else. This was the same energy as Top Gun: Maverick and it didn't have to leave the ground.

## 6. Sinners

The art. The visuals. The imagination. Ryan Coogler swings for the fences with this 65mm IMAX vampire blues musical set in 1932 Mississippi, and somehow connects on every pitch—delivering stunning visuals, a knockout dual performance from Michael B. Jordan as twin bootleggers, and Ludwig Göransson's phenomenal score that makes the Delta sound like the most dangerous place on earth. It's ambitious, genre-bending filmmaking that proves Coogler is operating at a level few directors can match. He's committed to Black Panther 3 and we'll be eagerly anticipating what else is to come from one of the most talented filmmakers of this generation.

## 7. Companion

Very top of mind for where our society is in terms of technological change and fear toward if things are moving too fast! Drew Hancock's wickedly smart AI thriller gives Sophie Thatcher a star-making role as an android navigating a twisted relationship, with twists sharp enough to make Black Mirror jealous. At a lean 97 minutes, it's the kind of genre filmmaking that respects your intelligence while keeping you guessing. I had a great time at this one and I think Jack Quaid is made for this type of role. The dark comedy is his home. Novocain was also good and I always look forward to seeing him in 'The Boys.' We need more of this type of movie.

## 8. Black Bag

Bow down once again to the excellence of Soderbergh… Steven Soderbergh delivers a master class in elegant efficiency—a 93-minute spy thriller so slick and witty it practically purrs, with Cate Blanchett and Michael Fassbender generating sparks as married MI6 operatives in David Koepp's razor-sharp script. Shot with Soderbergh's signature wide-angle style and scored by David Holmes, it's the kind of smart, sexy studio filmmaking they genuinely don't make anymore.

## 9. Predator: Badlands

Dan Trachtenberg pulls off the impossible again—making a Predator film from the Predator's perspective that actually works, with Elle Fanning delivering a haunting android performance that grounds the film's ambitious world-building. It's a bold franchise reinvention that proves sometimes the most interesting stories come from flipping the camera to the other side of the hunt. The franchise that was having some trouble re-inventing itself for a new generation has sprung to life with a triptych of different creations from Trachtenberg after 'Prey' in 2022 took it to a totally unexpected place and Predator: Killer of Killers surprised yet again in an animated format. There's much more to come in this franchise and I hope to see more movies from the director in other areas in the future.

## 10. Avatar: Fire and Ash

I'm a sucker for Avatar. The spectacle, the technology, the scope. It may have been rehashing of components of the last film and may not be on many top 10 lists come awards season, but for MY favorites, these will always be on… James Cameron continues his ambitious Pandoran saga with the third installment, delivering stunning 48fps visuals and introducing Oona Chaplin as the villainous Varang—a performance many critics have singled out as a genuine highlight amid the spectacle. At three hours and seventeen minutes, it's undeniably epic, and while some find Cameron running long on runtime, he remains unmatched at creating immersive worlds you want to get lost in.

## Honorable Mentions

### Train Dreams

LOTS of critics are putting this on their top 10. Personally, I didn't love it as much as the general consensus. I think part of it is I grew up very close to where this was shot and the landscapes are super familiar. Lots of reviews remark on the beautiful colors and cinematography, which are absolutely true, but to me, it felt like my childhood and every hike or weekend at the lake. It's so close, while filming, the cast and crew stayed in the same building where my dad lives in my hometown. In the film, Clint Bentley brought to life Denis Johnson's novella, with Joel Edgerton delivering a quietly powerful performance as a logger in the early 20th century Pacific Northwest. Will Patton's narration and Bryce Dessner's score wrap around the gorgeous landscape photography like a melancholy blanket. Netflix is pushing hard for a Best Picture and this may be their best chance in 2026.

### Bugonia

Yorgos Lanthimos finds his wickedly funny groove in this psychological thriller remake of the Korean cult classic Save the Green Planet!, pitting Jesse Plemons' paranoid beekeeper against Emma Stone's ice-cold pharma CEO in what Variety called "a duel—tactical, philosophical, brutal." Robbie Ryan's 35mm VistaVision cinematography is gorgeous in that unsettling Lanthimos way. They made corporate kidnapping feel like the darkest, funniest ideological cage match of the year. I liked it a lot.

### Rental Family

This one tore at the heart strings. Brendan Fraser's kind, soulful face has never been more perfectly deployed than in Hikari's warm dramedy about a struggling American actor in Tokyo who joins a "rental family" agency—a real Japanese service where actors play stand-in relatives for clients' emotional needs. Cinematographer Takurô Ishizaka bathes the story in bright daylight rather than the usual neon-soaked Tokyo palette. Fraser's quietly devastating performance is a beautiful, non-judgmental meditation on loneliness and connection. It's so great to see him back in films and the great comeback he's having.

---

The overall movie year of 2025 was a decent one. After the effects of the pandemic set things back for a few years, the 2023 writers strike and the 2025 Los Angeles fires have hit the community and the industry in so many ways. The 2026 slate is giant. Lots of franchise blockbusters and the return of Marvel and Star Wars to theaters. No predictions yet, but I'm hopeful!
