# Finding Your Podcast Concept

The difference between podcasts that thrive and podcasts that fade often comes down to the first decision: concept. Too many aspiring podcasters skip past this step, eager to buy microphones and choose theme music. They create "just another interview show" or "a podcast about everything I'm interested in" and wonder why nobody listens.

Your concept is your foundation. Get this right, and everything else becomes easier. Rush past it, and you'll struggle with every decision that follows.

## Start With Why, Not What

Before you think about format or topics, ask yourself why you're starting this podcast. Not the surface-level answer like "I want to share my expertise" or "I love having conversations." Dig deeper.

Are you building authority in your industry? Creating a community around shared interests? Processing your own learning in public? Entertaining people who share your sense of humor? Documenting a journey or transformation?

Your why shapes everything. It determines your content strategy, your monetization approach, your marketing tactics, and most importantly, your ability to sustain the show when motivation wanes. If your why is strong and authentic, you'll find the energy to keep going. If it's weak or borrowed from someone else's playbook, you'll quit.

Write down your why. Make it specific. "I want to help mid-career professionals transition into tech roles by sharing real stories and practical advice" is infinitely better than "I want to help people with their careers."

## Understanding Your Target Audience

Here's an uncomfortable truth: you cannot serve everyone. The podcasts that try to appeal to the broadest possible audience end up connecting with no one. Your job is to get uncomfortably specific about who you're serving.

Start by identifying one person. Not a demographic segment, an actual person. Give them a name, a job, specific challenges, particular interests. What do they listen to podcasts about currently? What questions keep them up at night? What would make them subscribe after hearing one episode of your show?

This isn't limiting—it's liberating. When you know exactly who you're talking to, content decisions become obvious. You're not second-guessing every episode topic or wondering if you're being too niche. You're serving your person.

Your ideal listener likely looks similar to you from a few years ago, or someone you've helped in your professional life. Mine that experience. What did past-you need to hear? What would have accelerated your journey? What would have made you feel less alone?

## Researching the Competitive Landscape

Many aspiring podcasters avoid researching existing shows in their space because they're afraid of discovering their idea isn't unique. This is backwards thinking.

You want competition. It proves there's demand. What you don't want is to be indistinguishable from that competition.

Spend time listening to the top 10-20 podcasts in your niche. Take notes. What do they do well? What gaps do they leave? What questions do they not answer? What audiences do they not serve?

Look for the whitespace. Maybe existing shows in your space are all interview-based, and there's room for a solo deep-dive show. Maybe they all target beginners, and intermediate practitioners are underserved. Maybe they're all serious, and there's room for humor.

Your competitive advantage might be your specific experience, your access to certain guests, your production style, your perspective, or simply your consistency in a space where others publish erratically.

## Choosing Your Format

Format is how you deliver your content. The main options:

**Solo Shows**: Just you and the microphone. Full control, consistent production, no scheduling dependencies. Requires comfort speaking alone and strong content planning.

**Interview Shows**: You plus guests. Built-in variety and borrowed audiences, but quality depends heavily on your guests and your ability to facilitate interesting conversations.

**Co-Hosted Shows**: You and one or more partners. Creates natural banter and different perspectives, but requires alignment on vision and schedule. Chemistry between hosts makes or breaks these shows.

**Narrative/Produced**: Heavily edited storytelling with multiple voices, music, and sound design. Most time-intensive format but can be incredibly engaging. Think documentary-style.

**Hybrid**: Combining formats—like solo episodes with occasional guest interviews. Offers variety but requires managing multiple workflows.

Choose based on your strengths, resources, and audience expectations. A solo show might be perfect if you're an experienced speaker with strong opinions. An interview format makes sense if you have access to interesting guests and love conversation.

Don't choose a format just because it's trendy. Choose what you can execute well consistently.

## Your Unique Angle

In a crowded podcast landscape, your unique angle is what makes someone choose your show over the thousand others in your category. This might be your specific expertise, your interview style, your production approach, your perspective, or your access to unique stories.

Maybe you're a lawyer who can explain complex legal issues in plain English. Maybe you lived through an experience that gives you credible insights others lack. Maybe your network gives you access to guests other shows can't book. Maybe your sense of humor makes dry topics entertaining.

Your unique angle isn't something you manufacture—it's something you discover by understanding what you bring that others don't. It's the intersection of your expertise, your personality, and your audience's needs.

Write a single sentence that captures your unique angle. Test it with people who know you. If they immediately nod and say "that makes perfect sense for you," you've found it.

## Common Concept Mistakes to Avoid

Before we move on, let's address the most common concept mistakes:

**The Everything Show**: Trying to cover too many topics dilutes your message and confuses potential listeners. Niche down until it feels uncomfortable, then niche down a bit more.

**The Vanity Project**: Creating a show primarily to hear yourself talk or feel important. Audiences sense this immediately. Lead with value, not ego.

**The Copy-Cat**: Recreating an existing successful show but "in your voice." If your concept is "basically [famous podcast] but me," you need to go deeper.

**The Trend-Chaser**: Launching a show based on what's hot right now rather than what you're genuinely passionate about. Trends fade; passion sustains.

**The Unclear Value**: If you can't articulate in one sentence why someone should listen to your show, your concept needs work.

Your concept should excite you and serve a real need for a specific audience. Get this right, and the rest of the process becomes much easier.
