# Recording Your First Episodes

You've got your concept, your plan, and your equipment. Now comes the moment that stops most aspiring podcasters: actually hitting record. There's something about that red button that makes even confident speakers second-guess everything.

Here's the secret: your first recording will feel awkward. Your second will feel slightly less awkward. By your tenth, you'll wonder what you were worried about. The only way through the discomfort is to start.

## Setting Up Your Recording Space

Before recording, optimize your physical setup. Small adjustments prevent hours of frustration.

**Microphone position:** Mouth level, about 6-8 inches away. Too close and you'll hear every mouth sound. Too far and you'll sound distant with noticeable room echo. Pop filter should sit 2-3 inches from the mic.

**Your position:** Sit or stand in a way you can maintain for 30-60 minutes. Slouching changes your vocal quality. If standing, many people project better and have more energy.

**Silence everything:** Phone on airplane mode. Close unnecessary programs. Tell people in your house you're recording. Turn off fans, AC, anything creating ambient noise. You won't notice these sounds while recording, but they'll be obvious in playback.

**Test recording:** Record 2-3 minutes, then play back with headphones. Listen for echo, background noise, microphone pops. Fix issues now, not after recording a full episode.

## Audio Best Practices

Speaking into a microphone requires adjustments from normal conversation:

**Consistent distance:** The most common mistake is swaying forward and backward while talking, creating volume fluctuations. Stay planted.

**Natural volume:** Don't whisper, don't shout. Imagine conversation with someone across a small table.

**Controlled breathing:** Breathe normally but be conscious of breathing away from the microphone slightly, or during pauses.

**Pacing:** Many new podcasters rush nervously or speak too slowly. Aim for conversational but slightly slower than natural speech.

**Mouth sounds:** Lip smacks and tongue clicks get amplified. Stay hydrated but not over-hydrated.

**Stay still:** Don't touch the microphone, desk, or cables while recording. These create thumps that are hard to remove.

## Solo Episodes

Solo episodes are just you and the microphone. Liberating because you control everything, but you're solely responsible for maintaining energy.

**Outlining vs. Scripting:** Full scripts ensure you say exactly what you planned but can sound stiff. Bullet point outlines feel more natural but require comfort with improvisation. Most successful solo podcasters land in the middle—scripted intro and outro, detailed bullets for main content.

**Practice out loud:** Your outline might flow perfectly in your head but stumble when spoken. Read through it once before recording.

**Energy management:** Without another person's energy to feed off, your natural voice might sound flat. Slightly exaggerate your enthusiasm. What feels over-the-top to you often sounds normal to listeners.

## Interview Episodes

Interviews add complexity but also built-in variety and energy.

**Guest preparation:** Send guests a brief overview: your show's audience, 3-5 topic areas you'll explore, technical requirements, and scheduled length. Don't send exact questions—you want natural conversation, not rehearsed answers.

**Technical coordination:** Ensure guests have decent audio. At minimum: quiet space, headphones, and stable internet. If they're recording on phone speaker in a coffee shop, reschedule.

**The pre-show conversation:** Spend 5 minutes before officially recording getting comfortable. This warm-up sets the tone and helps guests relax.

**Listen more than you talk:** Your job is to facilitate, not dominate. Ask a question, then actually listen to the answer. Follow up on interesting tangents. The best interview moments often happen when you deviate from your planned questions.

**Handle awkward moments:** If a guest gives a short answer, don't panic. Ask them to elaborate. If they ramble, look for a natural pause to redirect. You're steering the ship.

## Creating Intros and Outros

Your intro and outro provide consistency across episodes.

**The intro should:**
- Hook attention immediately (not "welcome to another episode of...")
- Establish what the show is about
- Tell new listeners why they should keep listening
- Be short (30-60 seconds max)

**The outro should:**
- Summarize key takeaways if appropriate
- Include a clear call-to-action (subscribe, review, visit website)
- Tease the next episode if possible
- Thank listeners sincerely

Record these once and reuse them, or record custom intros for each episode that tease specific content. Either approach works.

## Batch Recording

Recording multiple episodes in one session is more efficient than separate sessions:

- You're already set up and warmed up
- Your audio quality stays consistent
- You build momentum and rhythm
- You create buffer episodes for busy weeks

Three episodes is usually the sweet spot. The first gets you warmed up, the second hits your stride, the third requires conscious effort to maintain energy. Take real breaks between recordings.

## The Most Important Recording Advice

Your early episodes will not sound as good as the podcasts you admire. Those shows have years of practice, professional editing, and experienced hosts.

Your job isn't to match them immediately. Your job is to be good enough that your content value overcomes any production limitations, then improve with each episode.

Record your first episode. It will feel weird. Do it anyway. By episode ten, you'll have developed a rhythm. By episode twenty, you'll be genuinely competent.

The microphone is just a tool. It doesn't judge you. Your audience wants you to succeed. Hit record and start the repetitions that build expertise.
