# Planning Your Show

With your concept locked in, it's time to make the decisions that shape how your podcast actually works. Planning might feel less exciting than recording, but every hour spent here saves ten hours of confusion later.

## Naming Your Podcast

Your podcast name is working for you 24/7. It appears in search results, gets mentioned in recommendations, and sits in subscription feeds. It needs to work hard.

**Clarity beats cleverness.** A name that tells potential listeners what the show is about will outperform a witty pun they don't understand. "The Marketing Book Podcast" is boring but effective. You know exactly what you're getting.

**Check availability everywhere.** Before you fall in love with a name, verify the domain is available, the social handles exist, and no existing podcast has something confusingly similar. A quick search on Apple Podcasts and Spotify takes five minutes and prevents major headaches.

**Say it out loud.** You'll be introducing your show verbally thousands of times. Names that are hard to pronounce, spell, or remember create friction. Test it with friends who haven't heard you explain it.

**Consider SEO.** If someone searches for podcasts about your topic, will your name help or hurt? Including a keyword relevant to your niche (like "marketing" or "entrepreneur" or "parenting") aids discoverability.

Don't overthink this. A mediocre name with great content beats a perfect name with mediocre content. Pick something serviceable and move forward.

## Creating Your Show Description

Your show description appears in podcast directories and convinces potential listeners to try an episode. You have about 100 words before people stop reading.

Lead with value. What will listeners get from your show? Not what you talk about—what they'll gain. "Learn the strategies top entrepreneurs use to scale from six to seven figures" beats "A podcast where I interview entrepreneurs about business."

Include keywords naturally. People search for podcasts using specific terms. If your show is about productivity, the word "productivity" should appear in your description.

End with a call to action. "Subscribe and join thousands of listeners every week" or "New episodes every Tuesday" gives people a next step.

## Episode Length and Frequency

There's no universally correct episode length. What matters is consistency and delivering value efficiently.

**On length:** Shorter episodes (15-30 minutes) work well for tactical, focused content. Longer episodes (60-90 minutes) work for deep conversations and storytelling. The worst choice is padding 20 minutes of content into 60 minutes of rambling.

Let your content determine length. Say what you need to say, then stop. Respect your listeners' time.

**On frequency:** Weekly is the most common and expected cadence. Listeners build habits around weekly shows. Bi-weekly can work but requires clear communication. Daily shows exist but demand significant production capacity.

Choose a frequency you can maintain for at least a year. Consistency matters more than frequency. A reliable bi-weekly show beats an erratic weekly show that sometimes goes silent for a month.

## Building Your Content Calendar

Running out of episode ideas is avoidable with basic planning. A content calendar prevents the weekly panic of "what do I talk about?"

Start by brainstorming 50 episode ideas. Don't judge them—just list everything that could be an episode. You won't use all of them, but this inventory gives you options.

Categorize your ideas into buckets: foundational episodes that new listeners need, timely episodes connected to events or seasons, evergreen episodes that stay relevant indefinitely, and special episodes like interviews or series.

Plan at least 4-8 weeks ahead. Know what you're creating before you sit down to create it. This doesn't mean scripting every word—it means knowing your topic and key points.

Build in flexibility. Leave room for timely episodes when news breaks or opportunities arise. A rigid calendar that can't adapt feels like a prison.

## Production Workflow

Sustainable podcasting requires a repeatable process. Define yours before you start.

**Pre-production:** Research, outlining, guest booking and prep (if applicable), and gathering any resources you'll reference.

**Production:** Recording, which should happen in batches when possible. Recording 2-3 episodes in one session is more efficient than separate sessions.

**Post-production:** Editing, adding music and intros, exporting, writing show notes, creating promotional materials.

**Publishing:** Uploading to your host, scheduling release, distributing to platforms, and promoting on social media.

Map out how long each stage takes. Be honest—most people underestimate by at least 50%. A 30-minute episode easily requires 3-4 hours of total work when you include everything.

Identify bottlenecks. For most podcasters, editing is the time sink. Plan for it or budget for outsourcing it.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Before you launch, calibrate your expectations:

**Growth will be slow at first.** Most podcasts see minimal downloads in their first few months. This is normal, not failure.

**Your first episodes won't be your best.** You'll improve dramatically over your first 20 episodes. Accept that your early work is practice.

**It will take longer than you think.** Plan for podcasting to consume 5-10 hours per week minimum. If you're not willing to commit that, reconsider starting.

**Success looks different than you imagine.** Downloads are one metric. Impact, connections, opportunities, and personal growth often matter more.

Enter with clear eyes. Podcasting rewards patience and consistency, not overnight miracles.
